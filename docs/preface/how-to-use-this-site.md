# How to Use This Site

## Purpose

This page explains how the handbook is intended to be navigated and how its content should be interpreted. Because the site is not organized as a linear book alone, readers benefit from understanding the logic behind the repository structure and the relationships between pages.

## Three Ways to Navigate the Handbook

### 1. Navigate by Discipline

If your objective is conceptual understanding, move through the thematic sections such as **Requirements Engineering**, **System Architecture**, **Modeling and MBSE**, **Risk and Opportunity**, or **Verification and Validation**. This reading mode is useful when you want to understand a specific area of practice in depth.

### 2. Navigate by Lifecycle

If your objective is to understand when engineering activities occur and how they depend on one another, use the **Lifecycle** section as the spine of the handbook. This path shows how mission need leads to concept exploration, system definition, development, integration, operation, sustainment, and retirement.

### 3. Navigate by Case Study

If your objective is practical application, use the **Case Study** section and follow links back into the theoretical pages. This reading mode helps connect abstract methods to a realistic engineered system.

## How to Read Individual Pages

Most major pages are expected to follow a recurring internal logic. Although not every page will use identical headings, readers should generally expect to find:

- a statement of purpose
- the context in which the concept or process matters
- core concepts or workflow logic
- relationships to artifacts and governance
- links to relevant case-study applications

This consistency is deliberate. It allows the handbook to function both as a learning resource and as an operational reference.

## Interpreting the Case Study

The AIIDS case study is not intended to be a certification-ready or domain-complete system description. It is a controlled reference system used to demonstrate method, traceability, artifact relationships, and engineering reasoning. Readers should treat it as a pedagogical system with professional structure, not as a finished product definition.

## Interpreting Templates and Artifacts

Template pages describe the expected structure and role of an artifact. They are not intended to replace project-specific tailoring. In real work, artifacts must be adapted to contractual obligations, regulatory constraints, technical domain, organizational maturity, and review expectations.

## Interpreting Cross-Links

Cross-links are a key part of the handbook's architecture. A page about requirements may link to architecture, risk, and V&V because systems engineering decisions are interdependent. Readers should therefore treat the site as a **network of related engineering perspectives** rather than as a set of isolated chapters.

## Expected Evolution of the Site

The repository is designed to evolve over time. New pages may deepen the case study, add diagrams, expand MBSE content, introduce additional artifacts, or refine governance models. Readers using the site for real projects should therefore treat the repository as a living baseline, not as a static publication.
